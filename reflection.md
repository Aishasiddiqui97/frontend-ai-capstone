# Reflection — Draw the Path (Week 01)

Before this assignment, I thought a good portfolio needed many pages to impress recruiters — a blog to show depth, a skills page to list everything I know, and testimonials to prove my value. Auditing my workflow and designing a sitemap from scratch forced me to confront a harder question: does every page I include directly serve my goal of landing an internship? The answer reshaped how I think about portfolios entirely.

The most valuable lesson was learning to say no. Removing a blog, a standalone skills page, and a testimonials section was uncomfortable at first because they felt like "safe" additions. But when I measured each one against my proof statement and primary action, none of them earned their place. A blog builds authority over months — I need impact now. A skills page duplicates what project cards already show. Testimonials as a junior are aspirational, not authentic. Keeping the sitemap small forces every remaining page to carry its weight. That constraint actually improves quality, because I cannot hide weak content behind volume.

AI helped me pressure-test my own reasoning. When I reviewed the sitemap through Claude's critical lens, the feedback that surprised me most was that a recruiter might bounce if they cannot find a downloadable resume within seconds. I had buried the resume link inside the Home page content instead of surfacing it as a primary action. That blind spot — assuming visitors will read everything — is exactly the kind of assumption an external reviewer catches. I immediately decided to add a "Download Resume" button to the navigation bar and a prominent CTA on every page, not just Home and Contact.

Over the next eight weeks, I will use this Claude Project as both a tutor and an editor. Before I submit any deliverable, I will run it through the pressure-test lens — asking whether it supports my proof statement and primary action. I will also use the project to scaffold code for portfolio projects, generate commit messages, and review my own writing for clarity and impact. The goal is not to rely on AI for answers, but to build a habit of critical self-review with AI as a collaborative partner.
